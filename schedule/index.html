                  Monocultural, Redux

                  Category:iOS

                  Within five years many of us will be changing decade age. Some starting their twenties, others, their thirties and forties, some, their fifties. So, as each year passes, the chance that we will be working with someone who is significantly younger than us, increases. And, of course, it must also be true therefore, that as each year passes the chances of working with someone significantly older, increases. Or does it? If we look around at our community, if we look at the latest reports from Apple, Google, Facebook and others, what we actually see is an enormous skewed demographic. Why is that? Nature has always been strongest when it is at it’s most diverse…monocultures are a weakness that is destroyed through evolution. Yet our community still often favours a specific monoculture; young, white, males. We work in a time when the brogrammer moniker has given validity to a monoculture, where we have to have movements like #ILookLikeAnEngineer to remind us that we are not all the same, and when, despite a slew of films and TV shows about our community, it’s still rare to see any one who is of female or of colour. All this during an age when Brexit in the UK, Trumpism in the US and the rise of right wing parties across the world have normalised previously unthinkable levels of prejudice to those not like ourselves. This is not about ageism, racism, or sexism, this is about strength. How do we defend, evolve and cherish our community? How can we be open, welcoming, and supportive to everyone who wants to be a part of this amazing tech revolution? How, with small steps, can we be the change we want to be.

                  Michael May Wrisk, Lead iOS Engineer

                  Michael has been an iOS developer since just after the App Store launched, and various engineering incarnations before that (including Windows when NT meant New Technology and Java when it had a Mobile Edition). After stints at Shazam, The Guardian, Time Out, Songkick and others, he is now attempting to rethink the insurance industry for the always connected generations as the first mobile engineer at Wrisk, based in East London. An apparently infrequent speaker, he has ranged far and wide, having spoke on everything from DJ’ing after a terrorist attack (TEDx Alcatraz, 2010) to the many lessons he learned the hard way as a team lead (NSSpain 2015). By his own admission he is far too fussy about what coffee and beer he drinks, and very easily upset by bad user interfaces. (That should make the between talk breaks an interesting experience, then). Pulling together, and building on themes from his some recent talks - The Empathetic Programmer (UIKonf 2016) and Adding Tests To An Untested Project (CMD+U 2016) this talk will look not just at how one brings tests to a legacy code base, but why one should even try, as well as considering why they might not have been there in the first place. Expect empathy, humour, and plenty of real world code. If previous talks are anything to go by, there is likely to a few anecdotes too - probably set in, or around, the pubs of London. How does that work for you? Please don’t be afraid to tell me if you are not unhappy. I truly have no ego that needs stroking about this stuff and happily, gratefully, receive feedback. Always.

                  And then my phone became smarter: A Journey into the AWareness

                  Category:Android

                  François & Syrine will take a glance at the Awareness API and apply it to music recommendations. We are all carrying tiny computers with lots of sensors. With them, we can do more than display content, we can also make these devices aware of the world around us, making them smarter. One way to do this is to leverage the Google Awareness API in order to have rich features with minimal input from the user. It can trigger events when some conditions are put together: when you are close to a certain area, when you plug in headphones, when it is cold or sunny, etc. It can also be used to directly poll the sensors and get some information about the world around you. Using all these capacities to facilitate your life, your device will know what to do and when to do it, with or without your input (as configured by the user). In this talk François & Syrine will show you how to improve your music experience and take it to the next stage.

                  Building mobile apps with NativeScript

                  Category:Cross-platform

                  NativeScript is a free and open source JavaScript framework for building native iOS, Android, and Windows Phone apps. Add to that Angular 2.0 with TypeScript and you will get a truly amazing combination. But I know what you’re thinking: another way of building apps? What makes NativeScript special? Here are a few cool things: - Direct access to native APIs—no plugins required. Want to create a file on Android? Run new java.io.File()—in JavaScript! - Completely native performance through the use of a JavaScript bridge natively available on all three mobile platforms. - Cross-platform libraries for common use cases. Need to call a JSON API? Run http.getJSON() - Style native apps using CSS. Yep, that’s actually a thing. Curious about how it all works? Come learn! Architectures will be explained; apps will be built; and fun will be had by all. This talk is a mixture of slides and real life demo. I am going to explain what is NativeScript, how it works internally, what are the benefits, what is designed to do and show how to build a mobile app with the framework. After the talk the audience should know enough to get them started and where to find more information to build fully functional applications.

                  Make it count - Design & develop apps with Fuse

                    Cutting-Edge Responsive Web Design For Mobile

                    Category:Keynote

                    With HTTP/2, Service Workers, Responsive Images, Flexbox, SVG and Font Loading API now available in browsers, we all are still trying to figure out just the right strategy for designing and buildings responsive websites just in time. We want to use all of these technologies, but how can we use them efficiently, and how do we achieve it within a reasonable amount of time? In this talk, Vitaly Friedman, editor-in-chief of Smashing Magazine, will be looking into a strategy for crafting fast, resilient and flexible responsive design systems by utilizing all of those wonderful shiny web technologies we have available today. We'll also talk about dealing with legacy browsers and will cover a few dirty little techniques that might ensure that your responsive websites will stay relevant, flexible and accessible in the years to come.

Vitaly is the author, co-author and editor of all Smashing books. He currently works as editor-in-chief of Smashing Magazine in the lovely city of Vilnius, Lithuania.
